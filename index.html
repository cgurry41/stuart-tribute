Making the Kinfolk Proud: A Stuart Scott Story

By Colin Gurry

Most of the time, I hardly registered the anchors’ voices - just noise blending into the chaos. But this day, something stopped me mid-step as the SC Top 10 Plays flashed on the screen for the second time.

SportsCenter was the soundtrack to my childhood weekends growing up in the shadow of ESPN headquarters in Connecticut. From 9 a.m. to noon Eastern, it blared in the background as my brother and I shuffled around the house – scrambling for sneakers and jerseys, tripping over the dog.

“...coming in at number 5, Dwayne must have called Stephanie Gurry, ’cause the bank is open on a Sunday!”

Ain’t no way...

I halted in the doorway, half-dressed and ready to bolt for the car. My head snapped toward the screen just in time to see Dwayne Wade nailing a turnaround bank shot from the short corner. D-Wade is known the world over by any self-respecting basketball fan, but I was in one of the few households with 100% name recognition of Stephanie Gurry, the Banker.

I turned to my mom, who was still in the kitchen. “Did Stuart just mention you on SportsCenter?”

She chuckled as she looked over from the sink. “Ya, baby. I think he did. Such a goofball.”

For the first time in my intransigent teenage existence, the thought crossed my mind: My mom is cool.

Of course, Stuart Scott could do that.

On set of SportsCenter

Much has been written about Stuart Scott - the trailblazing ESPN anchor, pop culture icon, and the fighter who refused to let an eight-year cancer battle stop him from living to the fullest. But my small window into the Stuart Scott experience shows a man who believed his success and recognition were a privilege to be shared by the people in his corner.

Stuart and my mom had a seemingly unlikely friendship. A prominent African American TV personality from Chicago by way of Winston-Salem, North Carolina. A White bank manager and military brat who bounced around from Nebraska to Alabama, California, Iowa and Connecticut to name a few. One, a hip-hop fan who quotes Nas and Jay-Z on broadcasts. The other, a country girl who’s seen George Strait in three different cities. Demographic and professional characteristics would place Stuart a lot closer to Steph Curry than to Steph Gurry.

Biographies aside though, the foundation of their friendship, from the late 90’s to his passing in 2015, was simple. Shared geography, a smack-talking sports fanaticism, and above all, self-identification as a parent first and an anything-else second.

The friendship didn’t manifest in family outings or play dates (Though I do recall ducking behind office partitions in a near-empty bank trying to hide from my enemy commando – Stuart). It was in occasional meetings at the bank when Scott was in town, needling texts when the other person’s college team took a tough L, and the steady stream of braggadocious messages that chronicled the small triumphs of their favorite topics: their kids. We got updates on Taelor and Sydni’s dance competitions, art showcases, and honor roll achievements. Stuart got the admittedly purified version of my brother, Kevin’s, impressive ERA as a freshman pitcher, my snub from the All-Conference basketball team as a senior, and our college searches.

It must have been some combination of this inflated image of my talent and gratitude for those who paved the way for him. But when he heard I intended to try to walk-on to the basketball team at Georgia Tech, he phoned up second-year Tech Head Coach, Brian Gregory.

I have to imagine Coach Gregory, in the early stages of trying to restore a struggling team to its former glory, had bigger fish to fry than “a talented young player from small-town Connecticut and a really good kid.” Still, any media-savvy major Division 1 coach knows not to turn down a call from one of the most influential sports journalists on TV. He told Stuart I could try out and I would get a fair shot.

Weeks later, when I approached Assistant Coach Postorino’s office, I was pushing on an open door. I didn’t have to sell myself; I wasn’t an unknown potential waste of time. He gave me the time and place of the tryout and told me to come ready to play. The rest was up to me.

An introvert at heart, the life of a walk-on basketball player suited me. Early morning workouts, class, practice, homework, then back to the gym to get up shots. I didn’t have the skills or talent to genuinely compete with these future pros, but it didn’t matter. I got to stay in the gym, try to challenge them in practice, and learn from coaches with decades of experience.

On gamedays, my teammates suited up against basketball royalty like Duke and North Carolina. My role was more understated. Execute the opposing team’s plays during walk-through, dole out high fives, and keep the energy up on the bench.

Even so, I lived and died with the wins and losses of my rollercoaster freshman season of ACC basketball.

On a weeknight away game in March, I looked up at the scoreboard after Coach called our final timeout. 69-69 with 28 seconds to go. Largely on the back of freshman, Chris Bolden, we had just erased a 13-point deficit against 6th-ranked UMiami. Late in a season marked by hard fought close losses, we needed a reason to be optimistic heading into the post season tournaments.

That night in Coral Gables, Florida, Marcus Georges-Hunt became the reason.

The Georgia Tech walk-on door opens

Following his own missed floater, he tipped in the game-winning field goal with his left hand as time expired. I leapt onto the court to join the scrum of teammates suffocating our late game hero. It’s hard to describe the combination of elation, relief and camaraderie after a gritty road win, but it follows you into the locker room and onto the team bus.

An hour later, the adrenaline slowly wore off as I took my seat at the back of the coach bus carrying us to Miami International Airport. My thoughts drifted back to Atlanta, where a Thermodynamics make-up quiz and Calc II assignment awaited my return. That’s why I barely heard the ESPN highlights piping through the bus speaker system recounting our massive upset.

“… Chris Bolden hits the corner 3. All those practice reps with Colin Gurry must be paying off ‘cause that man was lights out tonight…”

Ain’t no way. My focus returned to the moment and I now recognized the distinctive voice anchoring the 11p.m. SportsCenter broadcast. Stuart Scott.

The bus went silent, and heads turned toward my seat in the back. Coach Gregory shot me a knowing smirk.

“Bro, respectfully, I had 21 tonight and you rode the bench. How in the hell are you getting the SportsCenter shoutout?” said Chris.

“No sweat, Chris,” I said. “If you need some extra work on your jumper, just let me know after practice tomorrow.”

Satisfied with a witty comeback, I eased back in my chair. For the first time in my college athletic career, the thought crossed my mind: Steph Gurry’s son might be cool too.

Ten years after his passing, Stuart still looms large. His legacy lives in Taelor and Sydni, his Memorial Cancer Research Fund, and a slight bend in the arch of sports journalism toward authenticity and joy. Of course, the ripple effects of his generous spirit also travel far.

Stephanie Gurry and I can attest: If you had the privilege of crossing paths with Stuart Scott, you might know what it’s like to be cooler than the other side of the pillow.
